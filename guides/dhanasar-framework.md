## The Legal Foundation

**Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016)** is a Department of Justice Administrative Appeals Office (AAO) decision that redefined how USCIS evaluates EB-2 National Interest Waiver (NIW) petitions.

Before Dhanasar, the evidentiary standard was ambiguous. Different USCIS field offices applied different thresholds. Some approved cases on thin evidence. Others rejected cases that seemed strong. The outcome depended partly on luck—which office adjudicated your case.

**Dhanasar changed this by establishing a clear, three-part test.** USCIS must find that:

1. Your work has **substantial merit**
2. Your work has **national importance**
3. It's in the **national interest** to waive the job offer requirement

Each pillar is measurable. Each pillar has specific evidentiary requirements. The framework applies uniformly.

### The Statutory Basis

The National Interest Waiver appears in the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), Section 203(b)(2)(B), which allows waiver of the labor certification requirement (and job offer requirement) for individuals whose work in the sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics "is in the national interest of the United States."

Before Dhanasar, USCIS used the **Kazarian test** (from Matter of Kazarian, 26 I&N Dec. 285 (AAO 2010)), which focused on:
- Extraordinary ability (a high bar)
- Or exceptional ability with national impact

**Dhanasar lowered the bar and clarified the test.** You don't need "extraordinary" ability. You need:
- Proven track record (substantial merit)
- Work addressing recognized need (national importance)  
- Clear commitment to continuing that work (execution capacity)

## The Three Pillars Explained

Dhanasar requires evidence on all three pillars. Weakness in any pillar creates RFE risk. Strength in all three creates approval confidence.

### Pillar 1: Substantial Merit

**Definition:** The alien's proposed endeavor has substantial merit.

What this means: You've already demonstrated—through your work history—that you can do what you claim. You're not a potential candidate. You're a proven performer.

USCIS looks for:
- **Track record of performance** in your field
- **Third-party validation** of that performance (publications, awards, citations, media coverage)
- **Scope of work** (Did you lead or assist? Were you central or peripheral?)
- **Measurable outcomes** (Not "I worked on X" but "I achieved Y")

### Pillar 2: National Importance

**Definition:** The alien's proposed endeavor has substantial importance in the United States.

What this means: Your work isn't just good—it matters to a significant population or solves a recognized problem.

USCIS looks for:
- **Documented need** (published research, government reports, industry analysis that establishes the problem is real and significant)
- **Scope of beneficiaries** (Does your work affect professionals, organizations, communities, or entire sectors?)
- **Currency** (Is this need current, not historical?)
- **Connection to your work** (Does evidence of national importance actually connect to what *you* do?)

### Pillar 3: Execution Capacity

**Definition:** The alien is positioned to advance the proposed endeavor.

What this means: You have a realistic plan, access to resources, and institutional support to continue your work in the United States.

USCIS looks for:
- **Proposed Endeavor clarity** (Specific, time-bound, measurable objectives—not vague aspirations)
- **Continuity** (Does your plan build on your past work or represent an unexplained pivot?)
- **Resource access** (Will you have time, funding, collaborators, institutional support?)
- **Realistic timeline** (Can this actually happen in the timeframe you claim?)

## Pillar 1: Substantial Merit

Substantial merit is about **proven capacity**, not potential. This is where many candidates stumble.

### What Evidence Counts

**Strong evidence of substantial merit:**
- Peer-reviewed publications (especially in high-impact journals)
- Citations to your work by other researchers
- Awards or grants (competitive selection)
- Patents (especially those actually used in industry)
- Media coverage in reputable outlets
- Keynote speeches at major conferences
- Leadership roles in research or production
- Work featured in textbooks or professional standards

**Weaker evidence:**
- Job titles alone ("I was a Senior Scientist")
- Self-published materials
- Internal company metrics
- Testimonial letters without third-party validation
- Educational credentials (degrees don't equal impact)
- Longevity (10 years at a job ≠ 5 years of impact)

### The Publication Question

One common misconception: Do you *need* publications to pass the substantial merit pillar?

**Answer:** Not always, but publications are the clearest third-party validation. If you have them, they strengthen your case dramatically. If you don't, you need *other* third-party validation (awards, patents, media coverage, citations by others).

The question USCIS asks: "Would an external evaluator—someone not you, not your employer—recognize your impact?"

If the answer is "Only if I explain it to them," you're in RFE territory.

### Common Substantial Merit Vulnerabilities

**Vulnerability 1: Experience ≠ Impact**
"I worked for 8 years at a research institute" is not the same as "My research was cited 150+ times and influenced industry standards."

*Fix:* Inventory your actual evidence. Citations, publications, awards, media mentions. Be specific and quantifiable.

**Vulnerability 2: Internal Validation Only**
"My company gave me a promotion" or "My boss thinks I'm excellent" is not third-party validation.

*Fix:* Look for external signals. Did your work get published? Cited? Covered in media? Selected for awards?

**Vulnerability 3: Confusing Scope with Impact**
"I managed a team of 20" or "I worked on a $5M project" tells you something about scope, not impact.

*Fix:* What was the *outcome*? Did the project succeed? Did the team produce something recognized? Did your management lead to measurable results?

**Vulnerability 4: Underdeveloped Metrics**
Self-reported metrics ("My solution saved the company $2M") without independent verification look suspicious to USCIS.

*Fix:* Use third-party metrics. If you have patents, cite the patent count and status (pending, granted, used). If you have publications, cite the count and citation metrics. If you have awards, cite the selection criteria and competitiveness.

## Pillar 2: National Importance

This is where many candidates miss the mark. They confuse "this is a good field" with "my work addresses national need."

### What National Importance Actually Means

National importance does *not* mean:
- Your field is trendy or growing
- You're well-known in your field
- Your work is "cutting-edge"
- It's obvious that your work matters

National importance *does* mean:
- There is **documented, published evidence** of a recognized need
- Your work **specifically addresses** that need
- The need affects a **significant population** or **important sector**

### How to Establish National Importance

**Step 1: Identify the Need**
What problem does your work solve or what gap does it fill?

Example problems:
- Healthcare delivery gaps in rural areas
- Cybersecurity threats to critical infrastructure
- Skills shortages in specialized fields
- Environmental challenges with no clear solutions
- Educational disparities in underserved populations

**Step 2: Find Published Evidence of That Need**
Search for:
- Government reports (GAO, HHS, NSF, CDC, Department of Labor reports)
- Academic research papers that document the problem
- Industry analyses from reputable consulting firms
- Congressional testimonies or policy papers
- White papers from established nonprofits

**Step 3: Connect Your Work to That Need**
Your work must *specifically* address the documented need, not just work in a field where the need exists.

**Example:**
- Documented need: "Rural healthcare has a 25% provider shortage" (published research)
- Weak connection: "I'm a doctor, which is a healthcare field" (too broad)
- Strong connection: "My telemedicine platform specifically addresses provider shortage in rural areas by deploying remote diagnostic support" (specific, addresses the documented need)

### The Citation Requirement

A common misunderstanding: Do you need to be cited by others to prove national importance?

**No.** But citations *help* prove substantial merit. For national importance, you need evidence that the *problem* is important, not necessarily that *you* are famous.

However, if your work is cited by other researchers or used by practitioners, that strengthens your case because it shows your work is solving the documented need.

### National Importance Vulnerabilities

**Vulnerability 1: Assuming Your Field = National Importance**
"AI is important to the nation" doesn't prove *your* AI work is important.

*Fix:* Point to a specific problem (bias in hiring algorithms, vulnerability in autonomous vehicles, etc.) and show how *your* work addresses it.

**Vulnerability 2: Vague Beneficiaries**
"My work benefits society" is too broad.

*Fix:* Be specific. Does it benefit healthcare professionals? Cybersecurity practitioners? Small businesses? Climate scientists? Identify the population clearly.

**Vulnerability 3: Conflating Trend with Need**
"Machine learning is growing rapidly" is trend data. It's not evidence of national need.

*Fix:* Look for structural problems or gaps that create actual need. Growth ≠ necessity.

**Vulnerability 4: No Published Evidence**
"It's obvious that [your field] is important" is not evidence.

*Fix:* Cite government reports, research papers, or industry analyses that *document* the need.

## Pillar 3: Execution Capacity

This pillar is about **commitment and feasibility**. Can you actually do what you're proposing?

### The Proposed Endeavor

The "Proposed Endeavor" is your commitment. It's not a description of what you've done. It's a description of what you *will do* in the United States.

**Characteristics of a strong Proposed Endeavor:**
- Specific objectives (not "advance the field")
- Measurable outcomes (publishable results, applied tools, new methodologies)
- Realistic timeline (12-24 months for major milestones)
- Resource identification (funding, collaborators, institutional support)
- Continuity with your past work (builds on your track record)

**Characteristics of a weak Proposed Endeavor:**
- Vague goals ("contribute to X field")
- No timeline
- Ambitious scope (too much for one person in reasonable time)
- No institutional support mentioned
- Pivots sharply from your past work without explanation
- Depends on factors you can't control

### Resource Access

USCIS wants evidence that you'll have:
- **Time:** Will you be employed full-time? Part-time? Self-employed?
- **Funding:** Do you have financial support? Employer backing? Grants?
- **Collaborators:** Who will you work with? Are they committed? (Letters of support help)
- **Institutional support:** Will you be affiliated with a university, research lab, company, or nonprofit?

### The Continuity Question

If your Proposed Endeavor is similar to your past work, the case is stronger. You have a track record of doing exactly this.

If your Proposed Endeavor is a significant pivot, you need to explain why you're qualified to make that pivot and why the new direction matters.

**Example of continuity:**
- Past: Published 15 papers on machine learning for medical imaging
- Proposed: Continue research on machine learning for medical imaging, focusing on diagnostic accuracy in rare diseases
- Why this works: Direct continuation of past work, proven capability, clear value add

**Example of problematic pivot:**
- Past: Worked in software engineering for fintech
- Proposed: Transition to consulting on climate change policy
- Why this creates risk: No track record in climate policy, unclear how past experience qualifies you, ambitious scope without proven expertise

### Execution Capacity Vulnerabilities

**Vulnerability 1: Vague Objectives**
"I will advance the field of biotech" is not an objective. It's a statement of ambition.

*Fix:* Specify what you'll produce. Research papers? Applied tools? New methodologies? Training programs?

**Vulnerability 2: No Resource Plan**
"I hope to find collaborators and funding" is not a plan.

*Fix:* Identify specific collaborators willing to work with you. Have preliminary discussions. Get letters of support.

**Vulnerability 3: Scope Creep**
Trying to accomplish too much in your Proposed Endeavor looks unrealistic.

*Fix:* Focus on 2-3 clear objectives for 12-24 months. You can do more later, but for the petition, be specific and achievable.

**Vulnerability 4: Disconnected from History**
"I've been a lawyer for 10 years, and now I want to transition to AI research" without explanation.

*Fix:* Either connect the dots (skills transfer, emerging interest clearly documented) or accept the risk and plan accordingly.

## Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: "I Need Extraordinary Ability"

**False.** Dhanasar explicitly rejected the "extraordinary ability" standard. You need substantial merit, not extraordinary ability. These are different things.

Substantial merit = proven track record of accomplishment in your field.
Extraordinary ability = the top 1-2% in your field.

Most approved EB-2 NIW cases involve candidates who are very good—not the world's best.

### Misunderstanding 2: "My Field Speaks for Itself"

**False.** Being a doctor, engineer, or AI researcher doesn't automatically establish national importance. You must connect your work to a documented need.

The question is not "Is your field important?" It's "Does your specific work address a documented need that matters to the United States?"

### Misunderstanding 3: "I Don't Need a Proposed Endeavor"

**False.** USCIS requires evidence that you're positioned to advance your proposed endeavor *in the United States*. This is a mandatory part of the test.

Your Proposed Endeavor doesn't need to be revolutionary, but it needs to be clear, realistic, and within your demonstrated capability.

### Misunderstanding 4: "Approvals Are Automatic if I'm Qualified"

**False.** Dhanasar is a framework for evaluation, not a guarantee. USCIS has discretion in how to weigh evidence. Presentation matters. Clarity matters. Documentation matters.

The most qualified candidate loses if they can't *prove* it to USCIS.

### Misunderstanding 5: "I Need to Work for a Top-Tier Institution"

**False.** Your employer doesn't determine your case strength. Your *evidence* does. A junior researcher at a major university with strong publication record beats a senior researcher at an unknown institution with no publications.

### Misunderstanding 6: "National Importance Means Household Name"

**False.** National importance means your work addresses a documented, significant need. You don't need to be famous. You need to solve a problem that matters.

## How Dhanasar Changed the Game

### Before Dhanasar: The Kazarian Era

Under the Kazarian test, USCIS looked for:
- **Extraordinary ability** (very high bar) OR
- **Exceptional ability** with evidence the alien would benefit the U.S. in an exceptional way

Different field offices applied this differently. No consistency. High rejection rates. Many strong candidates were denied.

The problem: "Extraordinary ability" is ill-defined. "Exceptional way" is subjective.

### After Dhanasar: The Three Pillars

The new test is more objective:
- **Substantial merit** (proven track record)
- **National importance** (addresses documented need)
- **Execution capacity** (realistic plan)

Each pillar has clear evidentiary standards. Approval rates increased. The framework is predictable.

### Why Dhanasar Matters

1. **Lower standard:** You don't need to be extraordinary, just accomplished and strategically positioned.
2. **Clearer metrics:** USCIS tells you exactly what they're looking for.
3. **Consistency:** Different field offices apply the same framework.
4. **Flexibility:** Your case strength depends on evidence, not on your institution or fame.

## Real-World Application

### Case Example 1: Scientist with Strong Publication Record

**Profile:**
- PhD in biomedical engineering
- 8 years post-doctoral research at mid-tier university
- 22 peer-reviewed publications
- 150+ citations to her work
- 2 patents pending (not yet granted)
- No major awards
- Proposed Endeavor: Continue research on tissue engineering for cardiac repair

**Dhanasar Analysis:**

*Substantial Merit:* STRONG
- Publication record is substantial (22 papers)
- Citation count shows external validation (150+ citations)
- Patents pending show innovation
- Research directly relevant to medical need

*National Importance:* MODERATE
- Cardiac disease is a documented national health problem (clear published need)
- Tissue engineering is recognized as a promising approach (published research)
- But: Her specific contribution to national importance isn't crystal clear
- Could strengthen by citing government health priorities or industry adoption of tissue engineering

*Execution Capacity:* STRONG
- Clear Proposed Endeavor (continue tissue engineering research)
- Direct continuity with past work
- Academic institutional affiliation provides resources and collaborators
- Realistic timeline for research milestones

**Classification:** MODERATE-TO-STRONG
Risk: RFE on national importance connecting to her specific work. Manageable risk.

---

### Case Example 2: Software Engineer with Product Success

**Profile:**
- 10 years software engineering at various tech companies
- Led development of a widely-used open-source tool (50k+ GitHub stars)
- No publications
- No formal awards
- Tool was adopted by 200+ companies
- Proposed Endeavor: Develop AI-powered features for open-source platform

**Dhanasar Analysis:**

*Substantial Merit:* STRONG
- Open-source tool is third-party validated (50k stars, 200+ company adoption)
- External validation is clear (not self-promotion)
- Track record of shipping products that people actually use
- No publications, but publications aren't mandatory—third-party validation via adoption is strong

*National Importance:* MODERATE-TO-STRONG
- Open-source development tools support software industry broadly
- The specific tool addresses efficiency gaps (well-documented problem)
- Adoption by 200+ companies proves practitioners recognize value
- Could strengthen with citations to industry reports on developer productivity

*Execution Capacity:* STRONG
- Clear Proposed Endeavor (AI-powered features for existing tool)
- Direct continuity with past work (open-source development)
- Growing adoption of tool suggests institutional support/community
- Realistic scope (focused feature set, not rewriting everything)

**Classification:** STRONG
Risk: Minimal. Clear evidence on all pillars.

### Case Example 3: Consultant with Industry Expertise

**Profile:**
- 12 years management consulting focused on healthcare technology
- No publications
- No patents
- Gave 3 keynote speeches at industry conferences
- Managed projects for 20+ healthcare organizations
- Proposed Endeavor: Help U.S. healthcare organizations accelerate digital transformation

**Dhanasar Analysis:**

*Substantial Merit:* WEAK-TO-MODERATE
- No peer-reviewed publications or external validation of specific accomplishments
- Keynote speeches (3) provide some external recognition, but limited
- Project work is real, but internal client validation doesn't equal third-party validation
- Missing: Did his work measurably improve outcomes? Get published case studies?
- Risk: USCIS will want external evidence of impact, not just client testimony

*National Importance:* MODERATE
- Healthcare digital transformation is a documented national priority (clear published need)
- But: His specific contribution to addressing this need is unclear
- Risk: Too generic—doesn't connect his specific expertise to the documented need

*Execution Capacity:* WEAK
- Proposed Endeavor is vague ("help healthcare organizations")
- No specific objectives, timeline, or outcomes
- No institutional affiliation mentioned (consultant? non-profit? research?)
- Risk: Looks like continuing consulting work, not a specific project

**Classification:** BORDERLINE-TO-WEAK
Risk: High. Would need significant strengthening. Recommend attorney consultation.

*How to strengthen:*
1. Publish case studies or research on your consulting work (builds substantial merit)
2. Articulate specific Proposed Endeavor with measurable objectives (execution capacity)
3. Find institutional partner (hospital, research center) to provide support and clarity

## RFE Patterns Under Dhanasar

USCIS commonly requests evidence on specific Dhanasar pillars. Understanding these patterns helps you preempt them.

### Most Common RFE: National Importance

**Pattern:** USCIS approves substantial merit but questions whether the work truly addresses a documented national need.

**Why:** Candidates often assume their field is important without providing published evidence of the specific need.

**How to preempt:** Cite government reports, peer-reviewed research, or industry analyses that document the need. Make the connection explicit.

### Second Most Common RFE: Proposed Endeavor Clarity

**Pattern:** USCIS asks for specifics about what you'll do, timeline, resources, and measurable outcomes.

**Why:** Vague language like "advance the field" or "continue research" doesn't meet the standard.

**How to preempt:** Write a detailed, specific plan. Include timeline, deliverables, collaborators, and resource access.

### Third Most Common RFE: Execution Capacity

**Pattern:** USCIS questions whether you have realistic access to resources (funding, collaborators, institutional support).

**Why:** Candidates don't document how they'll actually accomplish their plan.

**How to preempt:** Include letters of support from collaborators, employers, or institutions. Show preliminary commitments to funding or affiliation.

### Least Common RFE: Substantial Merit

**Pattern:** If your evidence is presented clearly, substantial merit is the least likely pillar to draw an RFE.

**Why:** Objective evidence (publications, patents, awards, citations) speaks for itself.

**How to strengthen:** Quantify your evidence. Don't just say "published papers"—say "12 peer-reviewed publications in top-tier journals with 85 citations."

## Strategic Implications

### Timing Strategy

If you're weak on one pillar, you have options:

1. **Wait and strengthen:** Publish papers, earn awards, build evidence (6-12 months)
2. **File and respond:** Submit petition, respond to RFE with improved evidence (12-18 months total)
3. **Consult attorney:** If complexity is high or time is critical

### Filing Strategy

- **Strong on all pillars:** File directly, high confidence
- **Strong on 2/3 pillars:** File with confidence, prepare for targeted RFE
- **Strong on 1/3 pillars:** Consult attorney, may need 3-6 months of strengthening
- **Weak on all pillars:** Not ready; focus on building evidence first

### Evidence Gathering Strategy

For each pillar, prioritize evidence that's:
1. **Third-party generated** (not self-promotion)
2. **Quantifiable** (numbers, metrics, counts)
3. **Current** (recent is stronger than historic)
4. **Specific** (not generic field-level claims)

## Final Thoughts

Matter of Dhanasar gave EB-2 NIW candidates a framework—clear, objective, and fair. The three pillars are testable. The evidentiary standards are knowable.

The candidates who succeed are those who:
1. Understand what USCIS actually looks for
2. Document their evidence rigorously  
3. Make explicit connections to the Dhanasar framework
4. Anticipate and preempt likely RFEs

You don't need to be the world's best. You need to prove you're accomplished, you're solving a recognized problem, and you're committed to continuing that work.

That's the Dhanasar standard. And it's achievable.
