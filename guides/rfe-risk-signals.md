# RFE Risk Signals: What USCIS Will Question

An RFE (Request for Evidence) is USCIS asking you to provide additional evidence to support your case. Not all cases get RFEs. Strong cases with clear evidence often get approved without requests.

But knowing what USCIS commonly questions helps you preempt these requests and strengthen your petition before filing.

---

## The RFE Landscape

RFEs occur when USCIS requires additional evidence to complete adjudication. They are common across all EB-2 NIW petitions. The strength and clarity of your initial filing directly affects the probability of receiving one. USCIS does not publish RFE rate data by category.

**RFE is not denial.** It's a request for clarification or additional evidence. Responding successfully to an RFE can result in approval. But responding poorly can result in denial.

---

## Category 1: Substantial Merit Risk Signals

### Signal 1.1: Weak or Absent Third-Party Validation

**What USCIS will question:**
"How do we know your work has impact? Where's the external evidence?"

**Red flags in your petition:**
- No peer-reviewed publications
- No awards or competitive recognition
- No citations to your work
- No media coverage or press mentions
- No patents
- No testimonials from recognized figures in your field

**Risk level:** MODERATE (not fatal, but requires mitigation)

**How to preempt:**
- Inventory all third-party evidence: publications, citations, awards, media mentions, patents, speaking engagements
- Quantify it (not "I've published papers" but "12 peer-reviewed publications in top-tier journals")
- If you have minimal third-party evidence, be strategic about what you lead with

**What if you don't have publications?**
You need *other* third-party validation. Options:
- Patents (especially granted, not just pending)
- Awards from competitive selections
- Open-source projects with substantial adoption/stars
- Citations to your work in others' papers or products
- Media coverage in reputable outlets
- Testimonials from recognized figures in your field (weaker, but counts for something)

### Signal 1.2: Limited or Unclear Scope of Work

**What USCIS will question:**
"Were you central to this work or peripheral? Did you lead or assist?"

**Red flags:**
- "Contributed to" without specifying contribution
- Large team projects where your role is unclear
- Internal work (not externally published or recognized)
- Job title inflation (titled "Manager" but no evidence of leadership impact)

**Risk level:** MODERATE

**How to preempt:**
- Be explicit about your role and contribution
- Example: "As lead researcher on the X project, I designed the methodology, conducted the analysis, and drafted the publication that received 50+ citations." vs. "I worked on the X project."
- Use metrics to show scope (publications authored, not co-authored; teams led, not participated in)

### Signal 1.3: Self-Reported Metrics Without Validation

**What USCIS will question:**
"Did you really achieve this, or are you inflating numbers?"

**Red flags:**
- "My solution saved the company $5M" (claimed by you, not verified)
- "I improved efficiency by 40%" (internal metric, not published)
- "My team grew from 5 to 20 people" (growth ≠ leadership quality)

**Risk level:** MODERATE-TO-HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Use third-party verified metrics
- If citing internal metrics, include documentation (audit, company statement, published case study)
- Better: Cite published case studies or external recognition of outcomes
- Example: "My work was recognized in Harvard Business Review case study [link] for achieving [outcome]"

### Signal 1.4: Outdated or Historical Evidence

**What USCIS will question:**
"This is from 2015. What have you done recently?"

**Red flags:**
- Most recent publication: 3+ years ago
- No recent awards or recognition
- "My major work was..."—past tense only
- CV with large gaps in recent activity

**Risk level:** MODERATE

**How to preempt:**
- Update your evidence inventory to include recent work (past 2-3 years)
- If you have a gap, explain it (sabbatical, career transition, family reasons)
- Emphasize recent accomplishments, even if smaller than historical achievements

## Category 2: National Importance Risk Signals

### Signal 2.1: No Published Evidence of National Importance

**What USCIS will question:**
"Why should we believe this is nationally important? What proof do you have?"

**Red flags:**
- No citations to government reports, research papers, or industry analyses
- Assumption that your field = national importance
- Vague claims ("this is important to healthcare")
- No documented need for your specific work

**Risk level:** VERY HIGH (this is the #1 RFE trigger)

**How to preempt:**
- Research published evidence of need in your specific area
- Cite government agencies (NIH, CDC, HHS, NSF, DOL, DHS, etc.)
- Cite peer-reviewed research documenting the problem
- Cite industry analyses (McKinsey, Gartner, etc.) if relevant
- Make explicit connections between published evidence and your work

**Example of strong evidence:**
"The National Institutes of Health identifies [condition] as a research priority area [cite NIH Strategic Plan]. My work directly addresses this need by [specific contribution]."

**Example of weak evidence:**
"My field is important to the nation."

### Signal 2.2: Mismatch Between Field Importance and Your Specific Work

**What USCIS will question:**
"Yes, AI is important. But is *your* AI work important?"

**Red flags:**
- "Machine learning is critical to U.S. competitiveness" (true, but generic)
- Your work is niche or specialized within a broad field
- Work that benefits only a small community
- Unclear how your work connects to the documented national need

**Risk level:** VERY HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Don't just discuss field-level importance
- Show how *your specific work* addresses the documented need
- Draw explicit connections between your work and the problem you identified

**Example:**
"Research shows that [published evidence] identifies bias in AI hiring algorithms as a critical problem affecting [population]. My work specifically addresses this by [your contribution]."

### Signal 2.3: Beneficiaries Are Too Vague or Too Narrow

**What USCIS will question:**
"Who benefits from this work? Is the scope broad enough to be 'nationally important'?"

**Red flags:**
- "Society benefits from my research" (too vague)
- Work that only affects your employer or small group
- Niche audience (experts in a sub-field only)
- No evidence of broader impact or adoption

**Risk level:** MODERATE-TO-HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Identify the beneficiary population specifically
- Show the population size or significance
- Cite evidence that the population recognizes the need

**Example:**
"My work benefits [specific population: healthcare professionals, cybersecurity practitioners, K-12 educators, farmers, etc.]. This represents [evidence of scale/significance]."

### Signal 2.4: Trend vs. Documented Need

**What USCIS will question:**
"Is this actually a documented need, or just a trendy field?"

**Red flags:**
- Confusing market growth with national need
- "AI is growing" (trend) vs. "There's documented shortage of X talent" (need)
- Cyclical technologies where need isn't persistent
- Hype without structural problems

**Risk level:** MODERATE

**How to preempt:**
- Focus on *structural problems* not trends
- Cite government reports identifying persistent problems
- Show that the need is documented, not speculative

## Category 3: Execution Capacity Risk Signals

### Signal 3.1: Vague Proposed Endeavor

**What USCIS will question:**
"What exactly are you committing to? How will we know if you did it?"

**Red flags:**
- "I will advance research in my field"
- "I will contribute to the development of new technologies"
- "I will collaborate with researchers"
- No measurable outcomes
- No timeline
- No institutional affiliation

**Risk level:** VERY HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Write a specific, measurable Proposed Endeavor with timeline
- Include deliverables that can be evaluated
- See the Proposed Endeavor guide for detailed help

### Signal 3.2: No Institutional Affiliation or Resource Plan

**What USCIS will question:**
"Where will you do this work? Who will fund it? Who will you work with?"

**Red flags:**
- "I will conduct research" (where?)
- "I hope to secure funding" (you don't have it?)
- "I will collaborate with researchers" (which ones?)
- No letters of support from institutions or collaborators
- No commitment of resources

**Risk level:** HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Secure preliminary institutional commitment (letter from university, lab, company)
- Identify specific collaborators and get their commitment
- Show funding sources (employer support, grants, personal resources)

**Example of strong institutional support:**
"I have secured a position as [title] at [institution], effective [date], with annual salary of $[X] and research budget of $[Y]. My supervisor [name] is committed to this project (see attached letter)."

**Example of weak institutional support:**
"I hope to work at a university and secure research funding."

### Signal 3.3: Proposed Endeavor Disconnected from Your History

**What USCIS will question:**
"Why should we believe you can do this? You've never done anything like it."

**Red flags:**
- Significant career pivot without explanation
- Proposed work in a field where you have no background
- No prior evidence of capability to execute your plan

**Risk level:** MODERATE-TO-HIGH (severity depends on size of pivot)

**How to preempt:**
- Show continuity between past work and proposed work
- If there's a pivot, explain the bridge
- Cite specific skills or experience that transfer

**Example:**
"While my prior work focused on X, the skills and expertise I've developed directly apply to Y. Specifically, [skills/expertise] are essential to Y, and I've demonstrated mastery of these through [prior accomplishments]."

### Signal 3.4: Overambitious Scope

**What USCIS will question:**
"Can one person realistically accomplish all this in 24 months?"

**Red flags:**
- Proposing work that would require a team
- Multiple independent projects listed
- Vague timelines that suggest indefinite open-ended work
- Scope creep (trying to prove importance by proposing too much)

**Risk level:** MODERATE

**How to preempt:**
- Focus your Proposed Endeavor on 2-3 specific, achievable objectives
- Show realistic timeline for completion
- If you have a team, specify team members and their roles

## Category 4: Overall Presentation Risk Signals

### Signal 4.1: Inconsistent Information

**What USCIS will question:**
"These two statements contradict each other. Which is true?"

**Red flags:**
- CV says one thing, narrative statement says another
- Different dates or titles in different documents
- Conflicting information about publications or roles
- Gaps or unexplained timeline issues

**Risk level:** MODERATE (damages credibility)

**How to preempt:**
- Review all documents for consistency
- Use the same job titles, dates, publication counts across all materials
- Explain any gaps or transitions clearly

### Signal 4.2: Missing or Weak Supporting Documentation

**What USCIS will question:**
"Where's the evidence for these claims?"

**Red flags:**
- Citing a publication but not including the actual publication
- Mentioning awards but not providing proof
- Referencing letters of support that aren't included
- Claims about citations without documentation

**Risk level:** HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Include copies of all publications cited
- Include awards, certifications, recognition documents
- Include letters of support from colleagues, mentors, collaborators
- Include evidence of citations (Google Scholar, ResearchGate, etc.)

### Signal 4.3: Using USCIS as a Fact-Checker

**What USCIS will question:**
"You're making claims we'll have to verify ourselves. Why isn't the evidence in your petition?"

**Red flags:**
- Assuming USCIS will Google your publications
- Assuming USCIS will know about your awards
- Not attaching evidence because "it's publicly available"
- Making claims without documentation

**Risk level:** MODERATE-TO-HIGH

**How to preempt:**
- Assume USCIS has never heard of you
- Include evidence for every claim
- Attach publications, awards, media coverage, letters
- Make it easy for USCIS to verify your claims without doing additional research

## The RFE Response Strategy

If you receive an RFE, you'll have 12 weeks to respond. USCIS will specify what evidence they're requesting.

**Response strategy:**
1. **Understand the RFE:** What specific evidence or clarification are they asking for?
2. **Assess the risk:** Is this salvageable? Do you have the evidence they're asking for?
3. **Gather evidence:** Respond comprehensively to each request
4. **Strengthen the narrative:** Use the opportunity to clarify any weak points
5. **Meet the deadline:** Submit 2 weeks early if possible

**RFEs that are often successful to respond to:**
- National importance (you can cite additional research and publications)
- Execution capacity (you can add letters of support or clarify your plan)
- Specific evidence requests (if you have the evidence)

**RFEs that are harder to overcome:**
- Substantial merit (if the evidence doesn't exist, it doesn't exist)
- Complete lack of documentation (hard to add significant evidence in 12 weeks)

---

## Preemption Is Key

The best RFE strategy is preemption. Build a petition so comprehensive and clear that USCIS has no questions.

This requires:
1. **Thorough evidence inventory** (What third-party evidence do you have?)
2. **Published research** (Can you cite research supporting your national importance claim?)
3. **Clear Proposed Endeavor** (Specific, measurable, realistic, grounded)
4. **Institutional support** (Do you have commitments?)
5. **Consistent presentation** (Does everything align?)

Address these before filing, and your RFE risk drops significantly.
