# EB-2 NIW FAQ: Real Questions from Candidates

These are real questions from EB-2 NIW candidates, answered based on the Dhanasar framework.

---

## On Substantial Merit

### Q: Do I really need publications to pass substantial merit?

**A:** No, but publications are the clearest third-party validation. If you have them, they strengthen your case significantly. If you don't, you need *other* third-party validation.

**Alternatives to publications:**
- Patents (especially granted, in-use patents)
- Awards from competitive selections
- Open-source projects with significant adoption (GitHub stars, downloads)
- Citations to your work by others
- Media coverage in reputable outlets
- Public recognition as an expert (speaking invitations, testimonials from recognized figures)

**The test:** Would an external evaluator—someone not you, not your employer—recognize your impact without explanation?

If the answer is "yes," you have sufficient evidence. If the answer is "only if I explain it," you're at risk for an RFE.

---

### Q: I've worked at a well-known company. Doesn't that count as evidence of impact?

**A:** Company prestige is background context, not evidence of *your* impact.

The question is not "Did you work at a top company?" It's "What did *you* accomplish that's externally validated?"

**Example:**
- Weak: "I worked at Google as a Senior Software Engineer"
- Strong: "I led development of [project] at Google, which was adopted by [scale] and recognized as [external recognition]"

Company affiliation can help if it provides context for your work, but it doesn't substitute for evidence of your specific accomplishment.

---

### Q: Should I cite my own publications in my petition?

**A:** Yes, absolutely. You should cite your own work as evidence of your accomplishment. But also cite:
- How many times others have cited your work
- Awards or recognition for your work
- Practical applications or adoption of your work
- External validation (media coverage, professional recognition)

Self-citation alone looks like self-promotion. Self-citation *plus* evidence that others recognize your work is strong evidence.

---

### Q: I have 50+ publications but very few citations. Is that a problem?

**A:** It depends. The answer is nuanced.

**High publication count + low citations suggests:**
- You're prolific but not highly influential
- Your work may be in lower-impact journals
- Your work may not have gained traction in the field

**This is RFE risk, but not fatal.** You can respond by:
1. Showing practical application (is your work used?)
2. Showing awards or recognition (are you recognized as an expert?)
3. Explaining publication strategy (are you in specialized venues with smaller but engaged audiences?)
4. Showing recent citation growth (maybe it's too early to assess)

If you have 50 publications and 2 citations total, this is a vulnerability. Address it proactively.

---

### Q: How many publications do I need?

**A:** There's no magic number. The question is not "How many?" but "Is your evidence compelling?"

**As a rough benchmark:**
- 5-10 publications in recognized venues = decent evidence
- 10-20 publications = good evidence
- 20+ publications = strong evidence

But a single publication in a top-tier journal with 100+ citations can be stronger than 15 publications in modest journals with minimal citations.

**Quality > quantity.** Always.

## On National Importance

### Q: I work on a very specialized problem. Can that still be nationally important?

**A:** Yes, absolutely. National importance doesn't mean broad appeal. It means addressing a documented, significant need.

**Examples:**
- You specialize in diagnosing a rare disease—if that rare disease affects thousands of Americans, and government/research organizations have identified the diagnostic gap as a problem, your work is nationally important.
- You specialize in cybersecurity for a specific infrastructure sector—if that sector is a national priority (energy, water, critical infrastructure), your work is nationally important.
- You specialize in improving education outcomes for a specific underserved population—if government reports document the educational gap, your work is nationally important.

**The key:** Find published evidence (government reports, research papers, industry analyses) that documents the need. Then show how *your* work addresses that specific need.

---

### Q: What if my field is obvious nationally important? Do I still need to cite sources?

**A:** Yes. Don't assume USCIS already knows your field is important.

**Example:**
- Weak: "Healthcare is obviously important to the nation"
- Strong: "The CDC identifies [specific healthcare problem] as a priority. My work addresses this by [specific contribution]. [Cite the CDC report]"

Always cite sources. Make it easy for USCIS to understand why your field matters *without having to do additional research*.

---

### Q: I work on a nonprofit project that helps underserved communities. How do I show national importance?

**A:** Show scale and impact:

1. **How many people does your project serve?** (or have potential to serve?)
2. **Is there published evidence of the need?** (government reports on underserved communities, research on the problem you address)
3. **Do established organizations recognize this need?** (citations from other nonprofits, government programs, research institutions)
4. **What's your measurable impact?** (communities served, outcomes improved, etc.)

**Example narrative:**
"My organization serves [X communities, Y individuals] in [region]. Government research identifies [problem] as affecting [population]. Our program has [measurable outcomes]. This work is nationally important because [cite evidence of need and impact]."

---

### Q: Is "helping my employer's business grow" national importance?

**A:** No. Employer success is not the same as national importance.

**National importance requires:**
- A *recognized problem* or *significant need* (documented in published research, government reports, etc.)
- Your work *specifically addresses* that need
- The need affects a *significant population* or *important sector*

**Example:**
- Weak: "I helped my company increase revenue by 30%"
- Strong: "I developed a solution that helped hospitals reduce patient wait times, addressing a documented healthcare delivery problem that affects [scale]. This work is nationally important because [cite published evidence of the need]."

## On Execution Capacity

### Q: How detailed does my Proposed Endeavor need to be?

**A:** Detailed enough that someone else could understand exactly what you're committing to and evaluate whether you did it.

**Test:** Could a skeptical evaluator read your Proposed Endeavor and say, "Okay, if you complete this, you will have done what you committed to"?

If yes, it's detailed enough. If you're hedging with words like "approximately," "hope to," "explore," or "potentially," you need to be more specific.

---

### Q: Can my Proposed Endeavor be different from my current job?

**A:** Yes, but with caveats.

If your Proposed Endeavor is different from your current work:
1. You need to explain why (career transition, applying skills to new domain, etc.)
2. You need to show capability (skills transfer, relevant background)
3. You need institutional commitment (employer or new institution backing this transition)
4. You need realistic scope (don't propose something entirely unrelated to your expertise)

**Example that works:**
"I've spent 10 years in healthcare IT. I now want to focus on healthcare AI, building on my technical expertise in data systems and clinical workflows. [University/company] has committed to supporting this transition."

**Example that doesn't work:**
"I've been a lawyer for 15 years. I now want to become a research scientist in genetics with no background in science."

---

### Q: Do I need to work for a major employer or university?

**A:** No. Your employer or institutional affiliation doesn't determine case strength. Your *work and evidence* do.

You can succeed as:
- An academic researcher (university)
- An industry researcher (tech company, pharma, etc.)
- A nonprofit researcher
- A consultant or independent practitioner (though this requires more careful planning around resources)

What matters is:
1. You have institutional support or a clear plan to work
2. You have access to resources (funding, collaborators, infrastructure)
3. Your Proposed Endeavor is realistic given your situation

---

### Q: What if I don't have an institutional affiliation yet?

**A:** You need to secure one before filing (or have a strong plan to).

**Options:**
1. Negotiate with a current or prospective employer for commitment
2. Approach a university and explore research affiliation
3. Join a research nonprofit or think tank
4. Freelance/consult, but you'll need documentation of how you'll fund your work and who you'll collaborate with

**Get a letter of support** from the institution or employer. This is much stronger than "I'm looking for a position."

---

### Q: Can I propose a startup as my Proposed Endeavor?

**A:** Yes, but with caution.

**What works:**
- You have a strong track record of successful product development
- Your startup builds directly on that expertise
- You have funding secured (or clear path to funding)
- You have collaborators or team members committed
- Your business plan connects to documented national need

**What doesn't work:**
- Startup is a pivot from your background
- No funding secured ("I hope to raise capital")
- Unclear how it addresses national importance
- Vague objectives ("build a tech company")

If you propose a startup, treat it like any Proposed Endeavor: be specific about what you'll build, how it solves a documented need, and how you'll fund it.

## On Timeline and Strategy

### Q: How long does this whole process take?

**A:** Typical timeline:

- **Preparation:** 2-12 weeks (depending on case strength and evidence gathering)
- **Filing:** 1-2 weeks (legal review and paperwork)
- **USCIS processing:** 12-18 months (median)
- **If RFE issued:** 12 weeks to respond, then 6-12 weeks for final decision

**Total typical timeline:** 18-24 months from start to approval.

---

### Q: Should I hire an attorney before filing?

**A:** Consider attorney help if:
1. Your case is borderline or weak
2. You have complex circumstances
3. You want professional confidence
4. You've never done this before

**You can likely DIY if:**
1. Your case is clearly strong
2. Your circumstances are straightforward
3. You're comfortable with administrative processes
4. You're willing to do detailed research

**Cost-benefit:** Attorney cost ($2k-$5k for petition review) vs. cost of weak petition (RFE, denial, reapplication delay).

---

### Q: What happens if I get an RFE?

**A:** USCIS will specify what evidence they're requesting. You have 12 weeks to respond.

**Common RFE requests:**
- Additional evidence of substantial merit (more publications, citations, evidence)
- Clarification and evidence of national importance
- Details on your Proposed Endeavor
- Documentation of institutional support

**RFE response strategy:**
1. Understand exactly what they're asking for
2. Provide comprehensive evidence
3. Use the opportunity to clarify weak points
4. Respond completely 2 weeks before deadline

**Success rate:** Responding well to an RFE often results in approval. Responding poorly or incompletely results in denial.

---

### Q: Can I file again if denied?

**A:** Yes, but you need to address the reasons for denial and strengthen your case.

**If denied:**
1. Get detailed feedback on weaknesses
2. Build additional evidence for 3-6 months
3. Consider attorney consultation
4. Refile with substantially stronger petition

**Cost of denial:** Time (12-24 months lost) + reapplication costs + emotional toll.

**Prevention:** Spend time now getting case right.

## On Specific Situations

### Q: I changed careers. Does that hurt my case?

**A:** Not necessarily, if you handle it well.

**What hurts:**
- No explanation for the transition
- Proposed Endeavor is unrelated to your background
- New field is unrelated to your expertise

**What helps:**
- Clear explanation of why you transitioned
- New field builds on transferable skills
- You have relevant background or training
- Proposed Endeavor grows from your combined expertise

**Example that works:**
"I spent 10 years in finance technology. I returned to school for climate science and now focus on climate tech—applying my financial systems expertise to climate solutions. My background uniquely positions me to [specific contribution]."

---

### Q: I haven't published in 3 years. Is that a problem?

**A:** It's a vulnerability. Address it.

**How:**
1. Explain the gap (career transition, administrative role, etc.)
2. Show recent accomplishments in other areas (awards, patents, product releases, leadership)
3. If planning to return to publishing, show how your Proposed Endeavor includes publication targets

Don't ignore a 3-year gap. Address it head-on with explanation and evidence of recent impact.

---

### Q: I'm an independent consultant. Can I still qualify?

**A:** Yes, but you need to be strategic.

**What you need:**
1. Clear, documented clients and project outcomes
2. Evidence of impact (client testimonials, case studies, publications, awards)
3. Proposed Endeavor with specific plans (continued consulting work, research, publications)
4. Plan for how you'll fund research/continued work (personal resources, client funding, grants)
5. Institutional partnership if possible (university, research center, nonprofit)

**Challenge:** No employer backing makes it harder to show resource access. Mitigate this with documentation.

---

### Q: I'm still early in my career (5 years experience). Can I qualify?

**A:** Yes, if you have strong evidence in that timeframe.

**What matters:**
- 5 years of *impact*, not just 5 years of work
- Publications, awards, recognition in that time
- Clear Proposed Endeavor
- Demonstrated expertise

**Examples of early-career strength:**
- Researcher with 8 publications, 80+ citations, 2 awards (strong)
- Software developer with widely-adopted open-source project, industry recognition (strong)
- Consultant with documented client success and growing reputation (moderate-to-strong)

**Examples of early-career weakness:**
- Recently finished PhD, minimal publication record or recognition (weak)
- Entry-level role with impressive title but minimal demonstrated impact (weak)

**The test:** Is the evidence of impact *compelling for someone early in their career*? Strong EB-2 NIW cases from early-career professionals typically have extraordinary evidence, not just a job title.

---

### Q: What if I did my best work outside the United States?

**A:** That's fine. Dhanasar doesn't require you to have done work in the U.S.

**What matters:**
- Your *work* (wherever done)
- Your *evidence* of impact
- Your *Proposed Endeavor* (what you'll do if waived)

Your past work can be international. Your proposed work should be in the United States.

---

## Final Thought

**The candidates who succeed are those who understand what USCIS actually looks for and document it thoroughly.**

You don't need to be the world's best. You need to:
1. Prove you're accomplished (substantial merit)
2. Show you're solving a recognized problem (national importance)
3. Demonstrate you're committed to continuing your work (execution capacity)

That's it. That's the Dhanasar test.
